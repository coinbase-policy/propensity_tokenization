Tokenized Stock Adoption Propensity Index

Which countries are most likely to adopt tokenized equities? Scored across six dimensions drawn from Chainalysis 2024, World Bank, IMF, and regulatory filings. Adjust the weights below to stress-test the rankings.

Retail Investor Participation

The share of adults who own investment products (stocks, bonds, or mutual funds). Higher participation means a population already familiar with equities — lowering the cognitive barrier to adopting tokenized versions. A country where no one invests in stocks is unlikely to adopt tokenized stocks, even if demand for foreign assets exists.

Metric: % of adults owning investment products. Sourced from World Bank Global Findex 2021 survey, supplemented with national exchange/regulator data (SEBI, B3, MOEX, DFM/ADX) where Findex figures are unavailable. Capped at 35%: score = 1 + 4 × (min(%, 35) / 35).

Source: World Bank Findex 2021 + National exchange data Capped at 35%

Demand from Restrictions

Pent-up desire for foreign equity exposure created by capital controls, FX conversion limits, or outright bans on foreign stock ownership. Captures latent demand that tokenized stocks could satisfy.

Metric: Chinn-Ito Capital Openness Index 2022 (ka_open). Inverted: more closed (CI = −1.856) → score 5; fully open (CI = +2.391) → score 1.

Source: Chinn & Ito (2006), updated 2022 Range: −1.856 to +2.391

Enforcement Weakness

How permissive or porous the state's enforcement of financial restrictions is. 5 = very weak enforcement (good for adoption), 1 = iron-fist control. High demand means nothing if the state can reliably shut down platforms.

Metric: Freedom House Freedom on the Net 2024 score (0–100, higher = more free). Mapped linearly: 0 → 1, 100 → 5.

Source: Freedom House 2024 Range: 0–100 (China=9, Argentina=72)

Crypto Propensity

Grassroots adoption of cryptocurrency as a proxy for willingness to use alternative financial rails. Countries already comfortable with crypto wallets and P2P transfers have lower friction to onboard to tokenized equities.

Metric: Chainalysis 2024 Global Crypto Adoption Index rank. Inverted: rank 1 (India) → score 5; rank 22 → score 1. Index weights on-chain value, P2P volume, and DeFi activity, all PPP-adjusted.

Source: Chainalysis 2024 Rank 1–22 (top 20 + estimated)

Currency Instability

Inflation pressure drives citizens to seek USD-denominated assets. Tokenized US equities are a natural hedge. Log-scaled to prevent extreme hyperinflation cases (Argentina 220%, Venezuela 190%) from distorting the full range.

Metric: World Bank CPI inflation 2024 (FP.CPI.TOTL.ZG). Log-scaled and capped at 300%: score = 1 + 4 × ln(1 + inflation) / ln(301).

Source: World Bank API Log-scaled, capped at 300%

Regulatory Openness

How welcoming the regulatory environment is toward tokenized assets, crypto, and fintech. Even high-demand countries need a permissive framework for platforms to operate legally. This is the only researcher-assigned score.

Metric: 1–5 scale assessed from TRM Labs Global Crypto Regulatory Tracker, CCAF Global Cryptoasset Benchmarking Study 2024, and national regulatory announcements as of Q4 2024. 5 = active licensing framework; 1 = blanket ban.

Source: TRM Labs / CCAF 2024 Scale: 1 (ban) to 5 (full framework)

How the composite score is computed

Each country receives a raw score of 1–5 on every dimension. All six dimensions are positive signals — higher always means better for tokenized stock adoption. Enforcement Weakness is pre-inverted in the data (score = 6 − raw enforcement capacity), so 5 = very permissive state, 1 = iron-fist control. Composite is always in the 1–5 range, with no negative values possible.

Score = Σ (weight_i × dim_i) ÷ Σ weight_i

Country Selection Methodology

The 18 countries in this index were selected from the Chainalysis 2024 Global Crypto Adoption Index top 20, which provides a principled, data-driven starting universe of countries where alternative financial rails already have meaningful traction. Starting from this list — rather than pre-selecting candidates — avoids the familiarity bias of relying on countries that appear frequently in English-language fintech media.

Three adjustments were made to the raw top 20:

  • Excluded developed markets — the United States and the United Kingdom appear in the Chainalysis top 20 but represent supply-side or institutional adoption stories, not retail demand driven by restricted access or currency instability. They were removed.
  • Retained hub markets — the UAE, Brazil, and Indonesia score lower on demand dimensions but were kept because they represent plausible platform or regulatory hub adoption pathways distinct from the demand-driven cases.
  • Added Venezuela — not in the Chainalysis top 20 due to data gaps, but included as a structural edge case: the combination of hyperinflation, near-zero functional stock market, and extreme capital controls makes it theoretically the highest-demand country in the world for USD-denominated tokenized assets, constrained only by platform delivery.
